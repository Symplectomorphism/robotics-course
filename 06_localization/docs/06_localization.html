Localization

  • GPS makes outdoor localization easy; unfortunately GPS is far from perfect.
    • relies on very weak radio signals received from distant orbiting satellites.
    • GPS cannot work where there is no line of sight radio reception.
  • GPS has only been in use since 1995 yet humankind has been navigating the planet for many thousands of years!
  • Dead reckoning is the estimation of location based on estimated speed, direction and time of travel w.r.t. a previous estimate.
  • Given average compass heading over the previous hour and a distance traveled the position at 33 p.m. can be found using elementary geometry from the position at 22 p.m.
    • Measurements on which the update is based are subject to both systematic and random error.
    • Modern instruments are quite precise but 500 years ago clocks, compasses and speed measurements were primitive.

Localization

  • Estimates are recursive: each estimate is based on the previous one.
    • errors will accumulate over time
    • for sea voyages of many-years this approach was quite inadequate!
  • The Phonecians were navigating at sea more than 4,0004,000 years ago and they did not even have a compass!
  • They navigated with crude dead reckoning but wherever possible they used additional information to correct their position estimate.
    • sightings of islands and headlands
    • primitive maps and observations of the Sun and the Pole Star.
  • A landmark is a visible feature in the environment whose location is known w.r.t. some coordinate frame.
    • A precise bearing measurement to two landmarks will therefore pinpoint our location, a process known as resectioning.

Localization

  • This process is critically reliant on correctly associating the observed landmark with the feature on the map.
    • Mistaking one lighthouse for another, e.g. 𝐂\bm{C} for 𝐁\bm{B} leads to significant error in estimated position (𝐪\bm{q} instead of 𝐩\bm{p}).
  • This is a very common error and countless ships have foundered because of this fundamental data association error.
    • This is why lighthouses flash with a unique flashing pattern!

Localization Problem

Find an estimate 𝐱̂\hat{\bm{x}} of the true, but unknown position, 𝐱\bm{x} of the robot. We also wish to know the uncertainty of the estimate (standard deviation associated with the position estimate 𝐱̂\hat{\bm{x}}).

  • It is useful to describe a robot’s estimated position in terms of a pdf.
    • allows for multiple hypotheses about the robot’s position.

Dead Reckoning

Dead reckoning

  • An odometer is a sensor that measures distance traveled and sometimes also change in heading direction.
    • For wheeled devices, can be determined by measuring the angular rotation of the wheels.
    • Direction of travel can be measured using a compass or
    • Change in heading can be measured using a gyroscope.
  • These sensors are imperfect due to systematic errors
    • incorrect wheel radius,
    • gyroscope bias
  • They are also imperfect due to random errors such as slip between wheels and the ground.
  • Robots w/o wheels, such as aerial robots, can use visual odometry based on observations of the world moving past the robot.

Modeling the vehicle

  • Let’s represent this succinctly as a 33-vector 𝐱=(x,y,θ)\bm{x} = (x, y, \theta) so that 𝐱⟨k+1⟩=(x⟨k⟩+δdcosθ⟨k⟩y⟨k⟩+δdcosθ⟨k⟩θ⟨k⟩+δθ) \bm{x}\langle k+1 \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} x\langle k \rangle + \delta_d \cos{\theta\langle k \rangle} \\ y\langle k \rangle + \delta_d \cos{\theta\langle k \rangle} \\ \theta\langle k \rangle + \delta_\theta \end{pmatrix} which gives the new configuration in terms of the previous configuration and the odometry.
  • Odometry is not perfect and we model the error by imagining a random number generator (vd,vθ)(v_d, v_\theta) that corrupts the output of a perfect odometer (sensor noise).

Robot’s configuration at the next time step, including odometry error

𝐱⟨k+1⟩=𝐟(𝐱⟨k⟩,δ⟨k⟩,𝐯⟨k⟩)=(x⟨k⟩+(δd+vd)cosθ⟨k⟩y⟨k⟩+(δd+vd)cosθ⟨k⟩θ⟨k⟩+δθ+vθ) \bm{x}\langle k+1 \rangle = \bm{f}\left( \bm{x}\langle k \rangle, \delta \langle k \rangle, \bm{v}\langle k \rangle \right) = \begin{pmatrix} x\langle k \rangle + (\delta_d+v_d) \cos{\theta\langle k \rangle} \\ y\langle k \rangle + (\delta_d+v_d) \cos{\theta\langle k \rangle} \\ \theta\langle k \rangle + \delta_\theta + v_\theta \end{pmatrix}

Estimating pose

Ship’s navigator problem

How to estimate our new pose given the previous pose and noise odometry?

  • The mathematical tool that we will use is the Kalman filter.
    • provides the optimal estimate of the system state assuming zero-mean Gaussian noise.
  • The filter is a recursive algorithm that updates, at each time step, the optimal estimate of the unknown true configuration and the uncertainty associated with that estimate.
  • The Kalman filter is formulated for linear systems but our model of the vehicle’s motion is nonlinear, so we’ll use the extended Kalman filter (EKF).
  • For this problem, the state vector is the vehicle’s configuration 𝐱=(xv,yv,θv) \bm{x} = \left(x_v, y_v, \theta_v \right) and the prediction equations 𝐱̂+⟨k+1⟩=𝐟(𝐱̂⟨k⟩,𝐮̂⟨k⟩)𝐏̂+⟨k+1⟩=𝐅x𝐏̂⟨k⟩𝐅x⊤+𝐅v𝐕̂𝐅v⊤ \begin{aligned} \hat{\bm{x}}^+\langle k+1 \rangle &= \bm{f}\left(\hat{\bm{x}}\langle k \rangle, \hat{\bm{u}}\langle k \rangle \right) \\ \hat{\bm{P}}^+\langle k+1 \rangle &= \bm{F}_x\hat{\bm{P}}\langle k \rangle \bm{F}_x^\top + \bm{F}_v \hat{\bm{V}}\bm{F}_v^\top \end{aligned} describe how the state and covariance evolve with time.

Estimating pose

𝐱̂+⟨k+1⟩=𝐟(𝐱̂⟨k⟩,𝐮̂⟨k⟩)𝐏̂+⟨k+1⟩=𝐅x𝐏̂⟨k⟩𝐅x⊤+𝐅v𝐕̂𝐅v⊤ \begin{aligned} \hat{\bm{x}}^+\langle k+1 \rangle &= \bm{f}\left(\hat{\bm{x}}\langle k \rangle, \hat{\bm{u}}\langle k \rangle \right) \\ \hat{\bm{P}}^+\langle k+1 \rangle &= \bm{F}_x\hat{\bm{P}}\langle k \rangle \bm{F}_x^\top + \bm{F}_v \hat{\bm{V}}\bm{F}_v^\top \end{aligned}

  • 𝐮̂⟨k⟩\hat{\bm{u}}\langle k \rangle: input to the process; in this case is the measured odometry, so 𝐮̂⟨k⟩=δ⟨k⟩\hat{\bm{u}}\langle k \rangle = \delta \langle k \rangle.
  • 𝐏̂∈ℝ3×3\hat{\bm{P}} \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3} is a covariance matrix representing the uncertainty in the estimated vehicle configuration.
  • 𝐕̂\hat{\bm{V}} is our estimate of the covariance of the odometry noise, which in reality, we do not know.
  • 𝐅x\bm{F}_x and 𝐅v\bm{F}_v are Jacobian matrices obtained by differentiating the equations of motion and evaluating the result at 𝐯=0\bm{v} = 0, giving 𝐅x=∂𝐟∂𝐱|𝐯=0=(10−δdsinθv01δdcosθv001)𝐅v=∂𝐟∂𝐯|𝐯=0=(cosθv0sinθv001) \begin{aligned} \bm{F}_x &= \frac{\partial \bm{f}}{\partial \bm{x}}\vert_{\bm{v}=0} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & -\delta_d \sin{\theta_v} \\ 0 & 1 & \delta_d \cos{\theta_v} \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \\ \bm{F}_v &= \frac{\partial \bm{f}}{\partial \bm{v}}\vert_{\bm{v}=0} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos{\theta_v} & 0 \\ \sin{\theta_v} & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \end{aligned} which are functions of the current state and odometry.

Localizing with a Map

Localizing with a map

  • Recall: uncertainty in position grows without bound using dead-reckoning alone.

  • The solution, as the Phoenicians worked out 4,0004,000 years ago, is to bring in additional information from observations of known features in the world.

    • In the examples, we will use a map that contains NN fixed but randomly located landmarks whose positions are known.

  • The robot is equipped with a sensor that provides observations of the landmarks w.r.t. the robot, described by 𝐳=𝐡(𝐱,𝐩i), \bm{z} = \bm{h}(\bm{x}, \bm{p}_i), where 𝐩i=(xi,yi)⊤\bm{p}_i = \left(x_i, y_i\right)^\top is the known location of the ithi^{\text{th}} landmark in the world frame.

  • To make this concrete, let’s consider a common type of sensor that measures the range and bearing angle to a landmark in the environment

    • such as radar or a scanning-laser rangefinder.

Observation of the ithi^{\text{th}} landmark

𝐳=𝐡(𝐱,𝐩i)=((yi−yv)2+(xi−xv)2arctan(yi−yv,xi−xv)−θv)+(wrwβ) \bm{z} = \bm{h}(\bm{x}, \bm{p}_i) = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{ \left(y_i - y_v \right)^2 + \left(x_i - x_v\right)^2 } \\ \arctan{\left(y_i-y_v, x_i-x_v\right)} - \theta_v \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} w_r \\ w_\beta \end{pmatrix}

  • Using the equation above, the robot can estimate the range and bearing angle to the landmark based on its own estimated position and the known position of the landmark from the map.
  • Any difference between the observation 𝐳♯\bm{z}^\sharp and the estimated observation indicates an error in the robot’s pose estimate 𝐱̂\hat{\bm{x}} – it isn’t where it thought it was!

Creating a Map

Creating a map

  • Somebody or something has to create the maps we will use.

Assumptions:

  • Sensor can determine the identity of each observed landmark.
  • The robot knows its own position perfectly (ideal localization).

  • We need to estimate the coordinates of the landmarks so for this problem, the state vector comprises the estimated coordinates of the MM landmarks we have observed so far: 𝐱̂=(x1,y1,x2,y2,…,xM,yM)∈ℝ2M×1. \hat{\bm{x}} = \left(x_1, y_1, x_2, y_2, \ldots, x_M, y_M \right) \in \mathbb{R}^{2M \times 1}.

  • The corresponding estimated covariance 𝐏̂\hat{\bm{P}} will be a 2M×2M2M \times 2M matrix.

  • The state vector has variable length since we do now know in advance how many landmarks exist.

    • Initially M=0M = 0 and is incremented every time a previously unseen landmark is observed.

Creating a map

  • The prediction equation is straightforward since the landmarks are stationary. 𝐱̂⟨k+1⟩=𝐱̂⟨k⟩𝐏̂+⟨k+1⟩=𝐏̂⟨k⟩ \begin{aligned} \hat{\bm{x}} \langle k+1 \rangle &= \hat{\bm{x}} \langle k \rangle \\ \hat{\bm{P}}^+ \langle k+1 \rangle &= \hat{\bm{P}} \langle k \rangle \end{aligned}

  • We introduce the function 𝐠(⋅)\bm{g}(\cdot), the inverse of 𝐡(⋅)\bm{h}(\cdot), which gives the coordinates of the observed landmark based on the known vehicle pose and the sensor observation 𝐠(𝐱,𝐳)=(xv+rcos(θv+β)yv+rsin(θv+β)) \bm{g}(\bm{x}, \bm{z}) = \begin{pmatrix} x_v + r \cos{\left(\theta_v + \beta\right)} \\ y_v + r \sin{\left(\theta_v + \beta\right)} \end{pmatrix}

  • Since 𝐱̂\hat{\bm{x}} has a variable length, we need to extend the state vector and the covariance matrix whenever we encounter a previously unseen landmark. 𝐱⟨k⟩′=𝐲(𝐱⟨k⟩,𝐱v⟨k⟩)=(𝐱⟨k⟩𝐠(𝐱v⟨k⟩,𝐳⟨k⟩)). \bm{x} \langle k \rangle^\prime = \bm{y}\left(\bm{x} \langle k \rangle, \bm{x}_v \langle k \rangle \right) = \begin{pmatrix} \bm{x} \langle k \rangle \\ \bm{g} \left(\bm{x}_v \langle k \rangle, \bm{z} \langle k \rangle \right) \end{pmatrix}.

    • This appends the sensor-based estimate of the new landmark’s coordinates to those already in the map.
    • The order of feature coordinates within 𝐱̂\hat{\bm{x}} therefore depends on the order in which they are observed.

  • An additional Jacobian that we will need later is the Jacobian for 𝐡(⋅)\bm{h}(\cdot) 𝐇pi=∂𝐡∂𝐩i=(1r(xi−xv)1r(yi−yv)−1r2(yi−yv)1r2(xi−xv)). \bm{H}_{p_i} = \frac{\partial \bm{h}}{\partial \bm{p}_i} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{1}{r}\left(x_i - x_v\right) & \frac{1}{r}\left(y_i - y_v\right) \\ -\frac{1}{r^2}\left(y_i - y_v\right) & \frac{1}{r^2}\left(x_i - x_v\right) \end{pmatrix}.

Localization and Mapping

Simultaneous localization and mapping

  • Feels like a ‘’chicken and egg’’ problem

    • we need a map to localize and
    • we need to localize to make a map.

  • However, based on what we have learned so far this problem is now quite straightforward to solve.

Pose Graph SLAM

Pose Graph SLAM

 

  • An alternative approach to the SLAM problem is to separate it into two components:
    • a front-end and a back-end
  • The robot’s path is considered to be a sequence of distinct poses.
  • The task is to estimate those poses.
  • Constraints between the unknown poses are based on measurements from a variety of sensors including odometry, laser scanners and cameras.

Pose Graph SLAM

  • The problem is formulated as a directed graph.
  • A node corresponds to a robot pose or a landmark position.
  • An edge between two nodes represents a spatial constraint between the nodes derived from some sensor data.

Pose Graph SLAM

  • The front-end adds new nodes as the robot travels as well as edges that define constraints between poses. E.g.:
    • When moving from one place to another wheel odometry gives an estimate of distance and change in orientation, which is a constraint.
    • The robot’s exteroceptive sensors may observe the relative position of a landmark and this also adds a constraint.
  • Every measurement adds a constraint – an edge in the graph.
    • There is no limit to the number of edges entering or leaving a node.
  • The back-end adjusts the poses of the nodes so that the constraints are satisfied as well as possible
    • The sensor observations are explained optimally.

Pose Graph SLAM

  • Coordinate frames {i}\{i\} and {j}\{j\} are associated with robot poses ii and jj.

  • We seek to estimate 0ξi{^0}\xi_i and 0ξj{^0}\xi_j in the world coordinate frame.

  • The robot makes a measurement of the relative pose iξj♯{^i}\xi_j^\sharp which will, in general, be different than the relative pose iξj{^i}\xi_j inferred from poses 0ξi{^0}\xi_i and 0ξj{^0}\xi_j.

    • We use it to adjust the poses of the nodes.

  • There is insufficient information to determine where the error lies

    • Naively adjusting 0ξi{^0}\xi_i and 0ξj{^0}\xi_j to better explain the measurement might increase the error in another part of the graph.
    • We need to minimize the error consistently over the whole graph.

Complementary filter

  • They provide a means to fuse multiple independent noisy measurements of the same signal that have complementary spectral characteristics.
    • μ1\mu_1: predominantly high frequency noise
    • μ2\mu_2: predominantly low frequency disturbance
  • Choose a pair of complementary transfer functions F1(s)+F2(s)=1 F_1(s) + F_2(s) = 1
    • F1(s)F_1(s): low-pass transfer function
    • F2(s)F_2(s): high-pass transfer function

  • The filtered estimate is given by X̂(s)=F1(s)Y1+F2(s)Y2=X(s)+F1(s)μ1(s)+F2(s)μ2(s). \hat{X}(s) = F_1(s)Y_1 + F_2(s)Y_2 = X(s) + F_1(s)\mu_1(s) + F_2(s)\mu_2(s).

  • This type of filter is also known as distorsionless filtering since the signal x(t)x(t) is not distorted by the filter.

  • Complementary filters are particularly well suited to fusing low bandwidth position measurements with high band widh rate measurements for first-order kinematic systems ẋ=u \dot{x} = u with typical measurement characteristics yx=L(s)x+μx,yu=u+μu+b(t) y_x = L(s)x + \mu_x, \qquad y_u = u + \mu_u + b(t)
